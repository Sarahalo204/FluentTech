### Rule 1: Definite Article Use
**CEFR Level mastered:** A1
**Why Arabic speakers struggle:** Arabic has a single definite article and no indefinite article distinction in the same way as English.
**Rule:** Use "the" for specific items already known to the listener, and omit articles for general plural nouns. Keep the structure simple in basic technical statements. 
**❌ Wrong:** I am writing the code for application.
**✅ Correct:** I am writing code for the application. (Use no article before general noun "code" and "the" before the specific application.)

### Rule 2: Indefinite Article Use
**CEFR Level mastered:** A1
**Why Arabic speakers struggle:** Arabic does not require an indefinite article in the same contexts English does. 
**Rule:** Use "a" or "an" before singular countable nouns when introducing something for the first time. Use "an" when the following word begins with a vowel sound. 
**❌ Wrong:** I installed an software update.
**✅ Correct:** I installed a software update. (Use "a" before consonant sound in "software".)

### Rule 3: Subject-Verb Order in Statements
**CEFR Level mastered:** A1
**Why Arabic speakers struggle:** Standard Arabic allows VSO word order, while English is typically SVO. 
**Rule:** In simple sentences, place the subject before the verb to describe actions clearly. This is essential for basic status reports. 
**❌ Wrong:** Finished I the task.
**✅ Correct:** I finished the task. (Maintain subject-verb-object order.)

### Rule 4: Simple Present for Habits
**CEFR Level mastered:** A2
**Why Arabic speakers struggle:** Arabic often uses present tense forms differently for general truths and habitual actions. 
**Rule:** Use the simple present for routines, general truths, and scheduled events at work. In tech, write "The server restarts" for regular events. 
**❌ Wrong:** The server is restart every night.
**✅ Correct:** The server restarts every night. (Use simple present for habitual action.)

### Rule 5: Present Continuous for Ongoing Actions
**CEFR Level mastered:** A2
**Why Arabic speakers struggle:** Arabic present tense can cover both ongoing and habitual actions, making English progressive forms harder to distinguish. 
**Rule:** Use present continuous for actions happening now or around now, especially when describing current work. 
**❌ Wrong:** I write the documentation now.
**✅ Correct:** I am writing the documentation now. (Use present continuous for current action.)

### Rule 6: Countable vs. Uncountable Nouns
**CEFR Level mastered:** A2
**Why Arabic speakers struggle:** Arabic does not categorize all nouns as countable or uncountable in the same way as English. 
**Rule:** Use no article with uncountable nouns like "information," "software," and add articles or numbers with countable nouns. 
**❌ Wrong:** I need a information about the bug.
**✅ Correct:** I need information about the bug. (Uncountable noun takes no article.)

### Rule 7: Prepositions of Time
**CEFR Level mastered:** A2
**Why Arabic speakers struggle:** Arabic time expressions often use different prepositions and structures. 
**Rule:** Use "at" for exact times, "on" for days, and "in" for months, years, and longer periods. 
**❌ Wrong:** The meeting is in Monday.
**✅ Correct:** The meeting is on Monday. (Use "on" for days.)

### Rule 8: Prepositions of Place with Technology
**CEFR Level mastered:** A2
**Why Arabic speakers struggle:** Arabic preposition equivalents do not always map neatly to English "in," "on," and "at." 
**Rule:** Use "on" for surfaces and screens, "in" for enclosed spaces, and "at" for a general point or location. 
**❌ Wrong:** The icon is in the desktop.
**✅ Correct:** The icon is on the desktop. (Desktop is treated as a surface in English UI context.)

### Rule 9: Past Simple for Completed Actions
**CEFR Level mastered:** B1
**Why Arabic speakers struggle:** Arabic perfect aspect differs from the English simple past. 
**Rule:** Use the past simple for actions that finished at a definite time in the past, especially when reporting yesterday or last week. 
**❌ Wrong:** I have fixed the issue yesterday.
**✅ Correct:** I fixed the issue yesterday. (Use past simple with a time reference.)

### Rule 10: Present Perfect for Experience
**CEFR Level mastered:** B1
**Why Arabic speakers struggle:** Arabic often expresses past experience without a dedicated perfect tense, causing confusion with English present perfect. 
**Rule:** Use present perfect to connect past actions with the present when no specific time is given, especially for experiences and changes. 
**❌ Wrong:** I worked on this feature before.
**✅ Correct:** I have worked on this feature before. (Present perfect indicates past experience relevant now.)

### Rule 11: Present Perfect vs. Past Simple
**CEFR Level mastered:** B1
**Why Arabic speakers struggle:** Arabic does not distinguish past events by reference to present relevance the way English does. 
**Rule:** Use past simple for completed past events with a time, and present perfect for indefinite past experiences or recent actions. 
**❌ Wrong:** I have deployed the update at 3pm.
**✅ Correct:** I deployed the update at 3pm. (Specific time requires past simple.)

### Rule 12: Third Person Singular -s
**CEFR Level mastered:** B1
**Why Arabic speakers struggle:** Arabic verbs do not require a suffix equivalent to English third person singular present simple. 
**Rule:** Add "-s" or "-es" to verbs in positive simple present sentences with he/she/it. 
**❌ Wrong:** The server respond to all requests.
**✅ Correct:** The server responds to all requests. (Add -s with third-person singular subject.)

### Rule 13: Question Formation with Auxiliaries
**CEFR Level mastered:** B1
**Why Arabic speakers struggle:** Arabic can form questions with intonation alone, without auxiliary verbs. 
**Rule:** Use auxiliary verbs such as "do," "does," "did," or "is" to create yes/no and wh-questions in English. 
**❌ Wrong:** You will send the report?
**✅ Correct:** Will you send the report? (Auxiliary verb before the subject in questions.)

### Rule 14: Subject Pronoun Required
**CEFR Level mastered:** B1
**Why Arabic speakers struggle:** Arabic often drops subject pronouns because verb forms indicate the subject. 
**Rule:** Always include the subject pronoun in English unless the subject is explicitly stated elsewhere. 
**❌ Wrong:** Am updating the code.
**✅ Correct:** I am updating the code. (Include the subject pronoun.)

### Rule 15: Using "Some" and "Any"
**CEFR Level mastered:** B1
**Why Arabic speakers struggle:** Arabic does not have a direct equivalent for the English quantifiers "some" and "any." 
**Rule:** Use "some" in positive statements and offers, and "any" in negative sentences and questions. 
**❌ Wrong:** Do you have some documentation?
**✅ Correct:** Do you have any documentation? (Use "any" in a question.)

### Rule 16: Compound Nouns Order
**CEFR Level mastered:** B1
**Why Arabic speakers struggle:** Arabic compound noun order can differ from English noun-noun constructions. 
**Rule:** In technical terms, place the modifying noun first and the main noun second (e.g., "user interface," "data packet"). 
**❌ Wrong:** interface user
**✅ Correct:** user interface. (Correct noun order for compound terms.)

### Rule 17: Gerunds after Prepositions
**CEFR Level mastered:** B1
**Why Arabic speakers struggle:** Arabic uses verbal nouns differently after prepositions. 
**Rule:** Use the gerund (-ing form) after prepositions, especially in phrases like "before deploying" or "after testing." 
**❌ Wrong:** Before to deploy, we check the code.
**✅ Correct:** Before deploying, we check the code. (Use gerund after "before".)

### Rule 18: Adjective Order
**CEFR Level mastered:** B1
**Why Arabic speakers struggle:** Arabic adjective order is more flexible and often follows the noun. 
**Rule:** In English, adjectives usually come before the noun, in common order: opinion, size, age, shape, color, origin, material, purpose. 
**❌ Wrong:** I reviewed a report technical.
**✅ Correct:** I reviewed a technical report. (Adjective before noun.)

### Rule 19: Using "Must" vs. "Have to"
**CEFR Level mastered:** B1
**Why Arabic speakers struggle:** Arabic obligation expressions do not map exactly to English modal verbs. 
**Rule:** Use "must" for strong obligation from the speaker and "have to" for external requirements or rules. 
**❌ Wrong:** I must finish the task because manager said.
**✅ Correct:** I have to finish the task because the manager said so. (Use "have to" for external obligation.)

### Rule 20: Present Continuous for Future Plans
**CEFR Level mastered:** B1
**Why Arabic speakers struggle:** Arabic present tense can express future meaning, so English progressive future forms are confusing. 
**Rule:** Use present continuous for planned future actions when time is specified. 
**❌ Wrong:** I start a meeting tomorrow.
**✅ Correct:** I am starting a meeting tomorrow. (Planned future action uses present continuous.)

### Rule 21: Conditional Type 1
**CEFR Level mastered:** B2
**Why Arabic speakers struggle:** Arabic conditionals use different particle systems and may not distinguish real future conditions. 
**Rule:** Use "If + present simple, will + base verb" for real future conditions, especially when planning contingencies. 
**❌ Wrong:** If we deploy, the system crashes.
**✅ Correct:** If we deploy, the system will crash. (Use future in result clause.)

### Rule 22: Conditional Type 2
**CEFR Level mastered:** B2
**Why Arabic speakers struggle:** Arabic often uses a single past form for unreal conditions, so English second conditional can be hard. 
**Rule:** Use "If + past simple, would + base verb" for hypothetical or unlikely situations. 
**❌ Wrong:** If we test it, we would find more bugs.
**✅ Correct:** If we tested it, we would find more bugs. (Second conditional for hypothetical situation.)

### Rule 23: Passive Voice for Processes
**CEFR Level mastered:** B1
**Why Arabic speakers struggle:** Arabic uses passive less frequently and with different auxiliaries. 
**Rule:** Use the passive to emphasize the action or result rather than the agent, especially in technical descriptions. 
**❌ Wrong:** The team updates the server automatically.
**✅ Correct:** The server is updated automatically. (Passive emphasizes the process.)

### Rule 24: Present Perfect Passive
**CEFR Level mastered:** B2
**Why Arabic speakers struggle:** Arabic perfect passive constructions differ from English present perfect passive. 
**Rule:** Use "has/have been + past participle" to describe actions completed with relevance to now, in passive form. 
**❌ Wrong:** The bug has fixed.
**✅ Correct:** The bug has been fixed. (Use present perfect passive with auxiliary "been".)

### Rule 25: Relative Clauses with "which"
**CEFR Level mastered:** B1
**Why Arabic speakers struggle:** Arabic can express descriptive clauses with different relative structures or omission patterns. 
**Rule:** Use "which" to add non-defining or defining information about a thing, especially in technical documentation. 
**❌ Wrong:** The server, which it handles requests, is stable.
**✅ Correct:** The server, which handles requests, is stable. (Omit repetitive subject after "which".)

### Rule 26: Relative Clauses with "who"
**CEFR Level mastered:** B1
**Why Arabic speakers struggle:** Arabic relative pronouns do not always distinguish people/objects the same way. 
**Rule:** Use "who" for people and "that" or "which" for things in defining clauses. 
**❌ Wrong:** The developer which fixed the bug is absent.
**✅ Correct:** The developer who fixed the bug is absent. (Use "who" for people.)

### Rule 27: Gerund vs. Infinitive after Verbs
**CEFR Level mastered:** B2
**Why Arabic speakers struggle:** Arabic verb patterns after certain verbs differ significantly from English. 
**Rule:** Learn verbs that take gerunds (e.g., "avoid testing") and those that take infinitives (e.g., "decide to deploy"). 
**❌ Wrong:** I suggested to postpone the release.
**✅ Correct:** I suggested postponing the release. (Use gerund after "suggest." )

### Rule 28: Article Use with Uncountable Technology Nouns
**CEFR Level mastered:** B2
**Why Arabic speakers struggle:** Arabic uncountable nouns and article usage do not always align with English restrictions. 
**Rule:** Do not use "a/an" with uncountable nouns like "software," "hardware," and "information" unless modified by a count noun. 
**❌ Wrong:** I need a software for testing.
**✅ Correct:** I need software for testing. (No indefinite article with uncountable noun.)

### Rule 29: Modal Perfect for Past Regrets
**CEFR Level mastered:** B2
**Why Arabic speakers struggle:** Arabic expresses past regrets differently and may not use modal perfect forms. 
**Rule:** Use "should have," "could have," or "might have" plus past participle to talk about past possibilities or regrets. 
**❌ Wrong:** I should fix the bug yesterday.
**✅ Correct:** I should have fixed the bug yesterday. (Modal perfect expresses regret.)

### Rule 30: Sentence Linking with "although"
**CEFR Level mastered:** B2
**Why Arabic speakers struggle:** Arabic subordination patterns differ and may omit conjunctions used in English. 
**Rule:** Use "although" to contrast ideas within a sentence; place the subordinate clause before or after the main clause. 
**❌ Wrong:** The deployment succeeded, although it had several errors.
**✅ Correct:** Although the deployment had several errors, it succeeded. (Use "although" for contrast.)

### Rule 31: Reported Speech Shift
**CEFR Level mastered:** C1
**Why Arabic speakers struggle:** Arabic reported speech often retains original tense, unlike English backshift rules. 
**Rule:** Shift tense back in reported speech when the reporting verb is in the past. 
**❌ Wrong:** She said the feature is ready.
**✅ Correct:** She said the feature was ready. (Backshift present to past after past reporting verb.)

### Rule 32: Using "few" vs. "a few"
**CEFR Level mastered:** B2
**Why Arabic speakers struggle:** Arabic quantifiers in negative and positive contexts do not always parallel English. 
**Rule:** Use "a few" for a small positive quantity and "few" to imply insufficient quantity. 
**❌ Wrong:** We have few tests left, so we can finish.
**✅ Correct:** We have a few tests left, so we can finish. (Use "a few" for positive meaning.)

### Rule 33: Using "still" vs. "yet"
**CEFR Level mastered:** B2
**Why Arabic speakers struggle:** Arabic adverbs of time often have different placement rules. 
**Rule:** Use "still" in affirmative sentences and "yet" in negative sentences or questions about unfinished actions. 
**❌ Wrong:** I have yet the report.
**✅ Correct:** I still have the report / I haven't sent the report yet. (Correct placement and use.)

### Rule 34: Verb + Object + Infinitive
**CEFR Level mastered:** B2
**Why Arabic speakers struggle:** Arabic does not always place the object before an infinitive in the same structure. 
**Rule:** Use this structure after verbs like "ask," "tell," and "want" when one action causes another. 
**❌ Wrong:** He told to me finish the documentation.
**✅ Correct:** He told me to finish the documentation. (Correct object before infinitive.)

### Rule 35: Using "despite" vs. "in spite of"
**CEFR Level mastered:** B2
**Why Arabic speakers struggle:** Arabic equivalents may not distinguish the preposition and noun forms clearly. 
**Rule:** Use "despite" before a noun phrase and "in spite of" with the same meaning; both contrast two ideas. 
**❌ Wrong:** Despite of the delay, we deployed.
**✅ Correct:** Despite the delay, we deployed. (Use "despite" without "of".)

### Rule 36: Conditional Type 0
**CEFR Level mastered:** C1
**Why Arabic speakers struggle:** Arabic general truths can be expressed differently from English zero conditionals. 
**Rule:** Use "If + present simple, present simple" for general truths and routine technical facts. 
**❌ Wrong:** If you reboot the server, it will always work.
**✅ Correct:** If you reboot the server, it always works. (Use present simple result for general truth.)

### Rule 37: Correct Relative Pronoun Omission
**CEFR Level mastered:** B2
**Why Arabic speakers struggle:** Arabic may omit relative pronouns more freely than English. 
**Rule:** In defining relative clauses, omit the pronoun only when it is the object, not when it is the subject. 
**❌ Wrong:** The engineer who I met yesterday is busy.
**✅ Correct:** The engineer I met yesterday is busy. (Omit object relative pronoun correctly.)

### Rule 38: Past Perfect for Sequence
**CEFR Level mastered:** B2
**Why Arabic speakers struggle:** Arabic often uses simple past forms where English requires past perfect for sequence. 
**Rule:** Use past perfect to show that one past action happened before another past action. 
**❌ Wrong:** When I arrived, the update finished.
**✅ Correct:** When I arrived, the update had finished. (Past perfect indicates earlier past action.)

### Rule 39: Avoiding Double Negatives
**CEFR Level mastered:** B2
**Why Arabic speakers struggle:** Arabic uses double negatives in some constructions where English does not. 
**Rule:** Use a single negation in English; do not pair "not" with another negative word in the same clause. 
**❌ Wrong:** I don't need no help with the deployment.
**✅ Correct:** I don't need any help with the deployment. (Single negation with "any".)

### Rule 40: Using "which" for Things
**CEFR Level mastered:** B2
**Why Arabic speakers struggle:** Arabic relative clauses do not always distinguish between people and things the same way. 
**Rule:** Use "which" for objects and ideas in non-defining clauses and sometimes in defining clauses when referring to things. 
**❌ Wrong:** The system which we designed is efficient.
**✅ Correct:** The system that we designed is efficient. (Use "that" or "which" correctly with things.)

### Rule 41: Inversion after Negative Adverbials
**CEFR Level mastered:** C1
**Why Arabic speakers struggle:** Arabic does not use the same inversion patterns after negative adverbials. 
**Rule:** Invert subject and auxiliary after expressions like "never," "rarely," or "only then" for formal emphasis. 
**❌ Wrong:** Never I have seen such a secure architecture.
**✅ Correct:** Never have I seen such a secure architecture. (Use inversion after negative adverbial.)

### Rule 42: Mixed Conditionals
**CEFR Level mastered:** C1
**Why Arabic speakers struggle:** Arabic conditional systems do not separate time frames with mixed clauses the way English does. 
**Rule:** Use mixed conditionals to link a past imaginary condition with a present result or vice versa. 
**❌ Wrong:** If we deployed earlier, we would catch the error now.
**✅ Correct:** If we had deployed earlier, we would catch the error now. (Use past perfect in the condition clause for a present hypothetical result.)

### Rule 43: Nominalisation in Formal Reports
**CEFR Level mastered:** C1
**Why Arabic speakers struggle:** Arabic frequently expresses abstract nouns differently and may overuse verbs in formal language. 
**Rule:** Use nominalisation to make formal technical assessments more concise, such as "the implementation of the feature" rather than "implementing the feature." 
**❌ Wrong:** We are optimizing the database implementation.
**✅ Correct:** We are carrying out the optimization of the database implementation. (Use nominalisation to create a more formal, report-style structure.) 

### Rule 44: Using "unless" for Negative Conditions
**CEFR Level mastered:** C1
**Why Arabic speakers struggle:** Arabic negation in conditional clauses typically uses different particles rather than a direct equivalent to "unless." 
**Rule:** Use "unless" to mean "if not" in a single conditional clause. 
**❌ Wrong:** I will deploy if not there are errors.
**✅ Correct:** I will deploy unless there are errors. (Use "unless" instead of negative if-clause.)

### Rule 45: Cleft Sentences for Focus
**CEFR Level mastered:** C1
**Why Arabic speakers struggle:** Arabic uses different structures to add emphasis, so English cleft sentences can be unfamiliar. 
**Rule:** Use "It is/was ... that" to emphasize one part of a sentence, such as the component or deadline. 
**❌ Wrong:** The bug is what we fixed last.
**✅ Correct:** It is the bug that we fixed last. (Cleft sentence for emphasis.)

### Rule 46: Subjunctive and Hypothetical Requests
**CEFR Level mastered:** C1
**Why Arabic speakers struggle:** Arabic has fewer subjunctive forms in subordinate clauses than English. 
**Rule:** Use the base verb after verbs like "suggest," "recommend," and "insist" in formal technical requests. 
**❌ Wrong:** I recommend that the team tests the patch.
**✅ Correct:** I recommend that the team test the patch. (Use subjunctive base verb after recommendation.)

### Rule 47: Reduced Relative Clauses
**CEFR Level mastered:** C1
**Why Arabic speakers struggle:** Arabic often uses full relative clause forms where English allows reduction. 
**Rule:** Reduce defining relative clauses by using participles when the subject is the same, for example "the server handling requests" instead of "the server that handles requests." 
**❌ Wrong:** The application that is updated nightly runs well.
**✅ Correct:** The application updated nightly runs well. (Reduced relative clause with past participle.)

### Rule 48: Advanced Passive with "get"
**CEFR Level mastered:** C2
**Why Arabic speakers struggle:** Arabic passive constructions do not generally use an equivalent of English "get" plus past participle. 
**Rule:** Use "get" in passive forms to emphasize a change of state or experience, especially in informal technical reports. 
**❌ Wrong:** The server got crashed by the patch.
**✅ Correct:** The server got corrupted by the patch. (Use "get" plus past participle to describe a change of state rather than a grammatical passive error.)

### Rule 49: Parallel Structure in Lists
**CEFR Level mastered:** C2
**Why Arabic speakers struggle:** Arabic coordination patterns allow mixed forms more readily than English parallelism standards. 
**Rule:** Keep verbs and clauses in the same grammatical form in a series to ensure clarity, especially in technical recommendations. 
**❌ Wrong:** We need to test the code, fixing the bug, and documentation should be updated.
**✅ Correct:** We need to test the code, fix the bug, and update documentation. (Use parallel verb forms.)

### Rule 50: Inversion in Formal Conditionals
**CEFR Level mastered:** C2
**Why Arabic speakers struggle:** Arabic does not commonly use inversion for formal conditionals in the same way English does. 
**Rule:** Use inversion after "had," "were," and "should" in formal conditional sentences instead of "if." 
**❌ Wrong:** If we were to review the architecture, we would avoid issues.
**✅ Correct:** Were we to review the architecture, we would avoid issues. (Formal inversion replaces "if.")
